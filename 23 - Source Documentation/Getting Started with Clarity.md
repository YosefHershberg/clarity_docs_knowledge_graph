## Objectives

Organizations can leverage the Objectives workspace to define objectives and key results that provide measurable outcomes to track progress. It also helps in proactive decision-making and resource allocation when organizations face strategic choices.

When you are looking to transform your organization, the first thing you need to do is define relevant objectives and key results. This will help you evaluate success and measure intermediate milestones.

To learn more about Objectives, see Working with Objectives.

## Hierarchies

Most large organizations have hundreds of investments spread across multiple business units. They often target diverse markets and customers. When you decide your organization's objectives and key results, you can use hierarchies to define parent-child relationships between investments and visualize associated dynamics and dependencies.

You can also leverage aggregation and calculation metrics to roll up data automatically. Then, you can view details, such as total capital cost, operational cost, and return on investment, for investments associated with each objective.

To learn more about hierarchies, see Create and Edit Hierarchies.

## Roadmaps

Roadmapping is a powerful planning tool that enables product managers to develop and communicate long-term product plans to business managers, engineering, and other stakeholders. You can use the roadmap to highlight how your current investments are tied to organizational objectives and strategies. You can also create new roadmap items that would be tied to new investments your organization would make to better align with its objectives.

To learn more about Roadmaps, see Clarity: Top-Down Planning with Roadmaps.

## Projects, Ideas, Custom Investments, and Teams

Most organizations have a core set of activities that are created and delivered by a majority of its employees. Clarity allows you to manage these activities by effectively providing four key investment types.
- Projects: Most large initiatives that have complex tasks that need to be managed every day by a group of people are called projects. Watch the following video to see how you can use projects in Clarity. To learn about Project Management, see Clarity: Create, Open, and View Projects.
- Ideas: When you are digitally transforming your organization, many employees will have creative ideas that align with your objectives. These ideas can be captured in Clarity, and a small group of people can work on them to create a viable prototype. Stakeholders can then decide if this idea has enough merit to be converted into a fully-fledged project. Watch the following video to see how you can use ideas in Clarity. To learn more about ideas, see Clarity: Capture, Develop, and Approve New Ideas.
- Custom Investments: Custom investments help align your investment nomenclature with your initiatives and strategies. They provide almost all the powerful capabilities you get with project management but also give you the flexibility to use terminology that resonates with your stakeholders. To learn more about Custom Investments, see Create and Edit Custom Investments.
- **Teams**: Since digital transformation often involves solving complex problems, organizations are pivoting to fund the teams delivering value, not the work itself. In this approach, teams are given persistent long-term funding and organized around products and value streams rather than in siloed departments. This helps teams work toward common, business-level objectives. To learn more about teams, see Working with Team Investments.

Getting Started with Digital Product Management
A recent Gartner keynote highlighted the change in how companies look at their businesses. Investment strategies are evolving to include funding of initiative-based work, such as programs and projects, and sustained assets, like platforms and products.
More companies are beginning to look at project and program investments as sustained assets, which we broadly can lump together under the generic term products. A lot of analysts talk about this as the move from projects to products.


This is not really a move from one to the other, but more of a shift in financial focus, recognizing that these assets need to be thought of differently. Defining a Product A digital product has the following characteristics: - A product is a sustained asset that has an indeterminate life. Some products may be short-lived, while others may
outlive expectations. However, most of the time, you don't know how long the lifespan will be when you begin. These investment vehicles can last for years or even decades. - A product delivers something of value that can be articulated in reasonable business terms. When you describe the value of a product, it should be obvious to the audience that the product has value. Examples of products include logistics apps, HR portals, and e-commerce sites. - A product receives explicit funding in the form of money or people through some channel. This characteristic explicitly differentiates products, which fall into the realm of investment decision making, from implicit products, which deliver value to people but never fall into the realm of investment decision-making. An example of an implicit product might be a wiki site that people like to use. The site is a sustained asset with an indeterminate life and delivers value that you can reasonably describe. Still, a generous individual may maintain it as part of their other daily responsibilities. Thus, one key question you can ask is whether certain implicit products should be made explicit and become a part of investment decisions.

Need for Digital Product Management Consider a scenario where you manage your personal investments, including stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. Would you ever manage all those assets in a daily spreadsheet? Every day you would look up and enter each investment price to see the overall portfolio value. Instead, you would rather turn to an investment app, such as Google Finance or Microsoft Money, where you have real-time portfolio information fused with news, events, and alerts, right at your fingertips? If these tools are available to optimize your personal investments, why wouldn't you want the same for your corporate and product investments? Would you want to manage people, work, and money in a spreadsheet? Watch the following video to learn more about the need for Digital Product Management.
Difference Between Projects and Products You can define a project as a marathon. With a marathon (like a major migration project), you decide to run the race, train for it, gather supporters who provide encouragement, and then, when the big day comes, you execute. When it's over, you declare victory (or defeat), then you (and your stakeholders) go back to life and on to other things. Sure, you may choose to run another race (conduct another migration), but that decision is fairly independent of this one, and the way you plan and execute for each event is to treat it as a special event.
A product is a league sport. With a league sport, the first key difference is that there is the expectation of many seasons (or, in the case of products, many releases). You have to plan not only for this match, but the next, and not only for this season but the next. Along the way, you have to make sure your fans (e.g., business stakeholders) stay engaged every season, so you need to deliver some incremental success for them. As the season (release) progresses, the game plan may change because you've got injuries (e.g., customer escalations) that disrupt your team and changes to the game plan to put you in a better position for the season (change in direction). When the season is over, you immediately begin preparations for the next season and work hard to ensure both fans and players stay engaged throughout the offseason (e.g., in-between releases).
Restructuring Your Organization to Manage Products Many organizations need to restructure existing roles and make some new investments as they embrace digital product management. The two key roles that get introduced are:
- Product Manager: A product manager runs or guides one (or more) products. They spend the majority of their time working with customers.
- Product Owner: Product Owners spend the majority of their time working with the engineers. They manage one or more aspects of the technical delivery of the product and maintain accountability to the Product Manager for technical delivery.

Prioritize Work Based on Business Outcomes
You've organized your investments. You've roadmapped your business objectives. The next step is to prioritize investments and funding allocation. But be aware, one of the biggest investment-planning issues companies face is prioritization. Usually, everything has to be done immediately. You can use Clarity to prioritize work based on business outcomes, not intuition and gut feeling. With strategic roadmaps, you immediately see how investment choices support both company and product strategies. You also see carryover items - incomplete work from previous periods that needs funding from this period - and mandatory investments in compliance, maintenance, and security, which can't be vetoed. Ultimately, however, your funding decisions will be driven by profitability. Personalize Your Avatar Image
To help users identify you, Clarity displays your personal avatar image. For example, the avatar for each user appears in a conversation. This circular icon appears in the top right corner of the application to identify you. By default, the image shows the initials for your first and last name. You can upload your own photo or an image.

> To differentiate between user sessions, you could use initials for contractors, photos for more personalized staff accounts, and color-coded graphics for special teams or department accounts. As an administrator, you might have several personal user accounts. For example, you use a photo avatar for your personal account. You use a series of multi-colored avatars for your test accounts.
Follow these steps:
1. Using a smart phone, web cam, scanner, or digital camera, capture your photo. Send the photo by email or copy it to your computer or a file server you can access.
2. In a mobile graphics app or a third-party desktop graphics application, edit and save an image file that represents you as a resource. For example, your organization might set up a color scheme or logos to identify users on various teams. You could also add text identifiers to avatars such as DEV, PMO, INFOSEC, RM, or BLUE TEAM. Follow these design requirements: a. Reduce the image file size to 250 KB or smaller. While five MB is the maximum, performance is best with smaller images. b. Large avatar images offer no benefit and simply waste disk space. A 150-dpi 8-bit color image is sufficient. c. Crop the image to square image dimensions of about 80 x 80 pixels. Give circular images a diameter of about 80 pixels. Larger images are automatically scaled down and cropped for best fit. However, large images might yield unexpected results when scaled. Also note that users might magnify a page. If your image is too small, it might appear blurry or pixelated. d. Supported image file formats include .png, .jpg, .gif, and .ico. Other formats such as .tif are not supported.
3. Log in to the Classic PPM Clarity. 4. Click the current avatar image at the top of the page and select SETTINGS. 5. Click UPLOAD. 6. Select the image file that you created in an earlier step and click Open. A preview of your image appears. 7. To upload a different image, click UPLOAD again. Creating Custom Help Content
The Custom Help Content feature in Clarity allows administrators to design and manage contextual Help that aligns with their organization's processes and standards. You can create custom help items that provide short guidance, link to internal documentation, or surface training videos directly within the Clarity Help panel. Custom Help Content can be targeted to specific product areas, ensuring that users see relevant guidance based on the page they are working on. For example, product managers working in Roadmaps can view organization-specific guidance for creating roadmap items, while staffing coordinators can see tailored instructions in the Staffing workspace.
> [!IMPORTANT]
> This capability is available for Clarity 16.2.1 and higher releases. Custom help content can be delivered to users in the Help panel in the following ways: - Custom Cards: Cards appear in the Help panel alongside standard Clarity Help topics. Users select a card to view additional content, such as internal best practices, policy guidance, documentation links, or training resources. - Custom Sections: Sections display directly within the Help panel without requiring user interaction. Sections are typically used for important instructions or guidance that should always be visible when users open Help for a specific page. - Custom Videos: You can create training or walkthrough videos that explain how to use a Clarity page according to your organization's standards and policies. These videos can be hosted on internal platforms such as Google Drive or other approved repositories and linked from a custom card or custom section.

Custom cards and sections can be added above or below the standard Clarity Help content, or they can be used to replace the default Help content entirely. This flexibility allows organizations to supplement Clarity guidance or enforce organization-specific processes. When designing custom help, it is best to start from what users see in Clarity and then map that experience back to ICE. The Help panel includes a Copy URL/UID option that copies the product area path (for example, /roadmaps ). This value identifies the page in Clarity and determines where your custom help content appears. The copied product area URL or UID is the link between the Help panel that users see in Clarity and the configuration that administrators maintain in ICE. You do not need to build every custom help item from scratch. Clarity allows you to create, edit, duplicate, copy, delete, replace, and import custom help content at both the product area level and the individual help item level. Filter Your Data You can use Filters to quickly identify records that match your selection criterion. Consider a scenario where you want to focus on ideas that have a significant impact on your organization. Hence you decide to focus on either high-priority ideas or ideas where the planned ROI is between 100 and 400 percent. In such cases, you navigate to the ideas grid and create a filter with Idea Priority and Planned ROI as the parameters and use the Match Any criterion. This criterion uses the logical OR filter to display ideas where the priority is high or the planned ROI is between the specified percentages. We could use the Match All criterion if we want to use the logical AND criterion. For selected string type attributes, the search filter textbox behaves like lookups to list the selected attribute results when filter options Is Equal To and Is Not Equal To are selected. From the resultant list, you can select multiple values based on the search text entered.

The displayed attribute values in the list honor the access permissions of the logged-in user. That is, you can see only the list of objects or sub-objects for which you have access permissions in the list that is getting displayed while using the Is Equal To or Is Not Equal To filter options. For example, assume that you have access to view only four projects. When you click the filter icon in the view, then you can see only those four projects in the Project list while using the Is Equal To or Is Not Equal To filter option. Working with Filter Groups
You can use the advanced filtering capability to define complex filter conditions. The filter layout is aligned to left and is available in Grid, Timeline, and Board layouts and the Staff Grid.

You can use this capability to: - Define advanced filter conditions with no code method. - Define filters in groups using Add Filter Groups where each group holds one of the multiple attributes. - Define individual attribute filter criteria within a group. - Filter on the same attribute multiple times. - Define if Any (OR) or All (AND) conditions should be used between each group or attributes listed in the specific filter
group. - Use Add Filter to add an additional attribute to be included in the filter group. - Use Add Group to add additional filter groups. - Remove individual attributes, the entire groups of filtered attributes, or all filter groups.
Below are some sample advanced filters: - (Template = No AND Active = Yes) AND ((Manager is Joyce Bauer or Cheryl Amos) OR (Sponsor is Beth Roberts) OR
(Project Type is Major Project))
- (Template = No AND Active = Yes) AND (Project name contains Compliance OR Security)
- (Template = No AND Active = Yes) AND (Sponsor is Beth Roberts OR Manager is Patrick Clark)
> [!NOTE]
> Some key points to remember are: - When defining filter grouping, you cannot use the same attribute in the same group. However, the different
groups can use the same attribute. - When adding a filter condition to an attribute, that was not previously filtered, using the columns header, the
column attribute will be added automatically to the first filter group. For example, if you already have three filter conditions, and you want to filter by Project Name using the column filter (column filter instead of using the Add Filter button), then the column filter condition will be automatically added to the first filter group. - You cannot use the column filter if it is active in multiple filter groups and cannot be displayed.
